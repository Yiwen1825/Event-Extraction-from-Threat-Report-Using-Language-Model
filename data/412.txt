It is – or it should be – a well known fact that attackers occasionally email potential victims with PDF attachments containing malware or exploit code. | O
But the latest attacks through PDF attachments are geared towards pushing users to enter their email account credentials into well-crafted phishing pages. | Phishing
Microsoft security experts saw a lot of variants of the same attack, and they all start with spoofed emails supposedly delivering asked-for documents. | Phishing
In one variation, the PDF makes it look like there has been an error, and the document can only be displayed with Microsoft Excel. | O
But instead of actually opening it with their own software, potential victims are urged to open it by following the link offered in the PDF: | O
If they do that, they will be redirected to a web page that makes it seem like the document can only be opened if the user signs in with their email credentials. | O
In another variant, the PDF urges users to click on a link that will supposedly allow them to view a Dropbox-hosted document online. | O
“Social engineering attacks are designed to take advantage of possible lapses in decision-making. | O
Awareness is key; that is why we’re making these cybercriminal tactics known,” Microsoft’s Alden Pornasdoro explained. | O
“In these times, when we’re seeing heightened phishing attacks with improved social engineering techniques, a little bit of paranoia doesn’t hurt. | Phishing
